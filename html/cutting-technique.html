Importance of cutting quality gum

Providing koalas with a quality, varied species diet is one of the most important aspects of keeping Koala in captivity.
Our goal at Cleland is to house a collection of Koalas for the purpose of public education, tourism, research and breeding.
Delivering premium gum to our koalas helps ensure good koala health, well-being and behaviour.

Plantation cutting

The aim of pollarding our plantation trees is to get the tree to produce and grow leaf rather than wood.
This involves regular and heavy pruning encourages this younger leaf growth.
In all the plantations we pollard the trees once they grow to approx. 20 to 25 cm diameter.




Pollarding a tree

To make the initial pollard cut use a sharp chainsaw to make a scarf cut in the tree to guard against tearing the bark on the tree.
Make the cut at waist height facing North, be sure to have an angle on the cut to ensure good water drainage. (approx.30 to 40 degrees.




Be sure that the newly pollarded tree has good light access, to ensure good regrowth.







Pruning a pollarded tree

After approx. 12 months (depending on light access and weather conditions) the newly pollarded tree has had a flush of new growth with many new leaders.









To encourage the tree to produce productive gum pieces for koala feed we then prune up the new growth to have anything from 7 to 10 leaders and discard all other growth. Once pruned is now left for another 12 to 18 months.













Ready to harvest

After approx. 12 to 18 month the pollarded tree should be ready to harvest, with 7 to 10 strong branches approximately 10 to 15 centimetres in diameter.













In a well-planned and maintained plantation it is possible to drive Fauna 2 along the plantation rows. Able to cut the trees and easily place the pieces straight on the ute.







When harvesting cut the branches flush back to the initial pollarded stump, in readiness for the regrowth to start growing and the leaf production process starts again.









How not to cut the trees and why



When pollarding a tree, it is important to ensure none of the bark around the circumference of the tree is ripped. The tree will dry out and struggle. The cut is left rough and open, water will sit in these crevices and eventually rot out the tree causing the tree not to reshoot and die.





What will potentially happen here is the tree will put its energy into the side straggly pieces of gum that have been left behind and will not put all its energy into a flush of new growth.
Please ensure when repollarding trees that every branch and twig is cut flush back to the tree stump.







When harvesting a pollarded tree be ruthless and take the all the leader branches.





This tree has been pollarded but the cuts need to be much closer to the stump. If left like this the protruding wood can dry out and not reshoot. It is safer to cut as close back to the stump as possible.







An example of a tree just lopped off without much after thought. Ultimately it is too small in diameter to pollard. But now it has been cut, the rest of the branches need to back all cut back to the trunk to encourage new growth.





The cut protruding wood will potentially die and the one leader that is left will potentially be slow growing. To fix it would be best to recut al cuts back to the stump along with the one leader that was left.









Good housekeeping



Please do not leave off cuts laying around the plantations.



Always stack offcuts into neat piles and place on track edge for readiness to be picked up to burn off later. If too heavy or too far from the track edge, do you best to stack in an open area to be burn where is or make piles parallel with the plantation rows to enable slashers to still have row access. Please ask if unsure.







Cutting in Conservation Parks


Please be mindful when cutting in Conservation parks all cuts need to be low to the ground flush back to the stump, inconspicuous cutting unlike the plantation pollard cuts.



Cutting on Forestry Land in between Pine plantations


When cutting and clearing our red gum saplings in amongst the pine plantations on Forestry land, please be sure to cut the saplings flush to the ground to avoid making tripping hazards.





Under power lines cutting


Cleland has a lot of gum cutting areas directly under powerlines. Some of these areas are somewhat opportunistic and are best to cleared out before the ETSA gum cutters come through and do the annual fire safety cut normally in springtime, which means we lose all the gum if we havenâ€™t kept it low enough under the power lines. Please keep a close eye on all under powerline areas and ensure that the gum does not get too high.



Track edges cutting


Track edge cutting is used to keep the tracks clear for fire safety in Conservation parks and Forestry Areas. Please cut trees low to the ground (inconspicuous cutting).


Habitat for Wildlife


Please be mindful that the plantation trees are utilised by the local wildlife, sometimes you will have to work around them and leave trees that are being used as resting and nesting spots.





Length and quality of gum pieces


When cutting the gum into feed pieces it is important to cut them to a manageable size for all staff to handle safely and comfortably. It is inefficient if the pieces are cut too long or too short.
When pieces are cut too long the keepers have trouble putting the gum in and out of the gum holding pots with ease and normally these pieces must be recut which is time wasting.
When pieces are cut to short the koalas have trouble getting to the gum.
The height to aim for a good piece of koala feed gum is approx. between 150 to 180 cm in length.
The height to aim for tip is approx. 150 cm in length. It is vital that only good quality gum is bought back to the park to feed out to the koalas.





If unsure of gum quality, please check with the Senior Koala Keeper.









Collecting Tips


Keeping up a good supply of gum tip for the handling sessions and training of the koalas is of the highest importance. It can make or break a day for the Koala Keepers working with the koalas as good quality tip is the catalyst to providing the best koala experience for our park guests and a positive experience for out koalas.





When harvesting gum always be on the lookout for good tip. During the Winter months it is challenging to find new growth tip and tend to reply heavily on Platypus and Swamp Yate which the koalas seem to like all year round.
When collecting tip but not wanting to cut the whole trees down, it is possible just to snip the tops out of the tree to get the tippy tops.







Feeding out of Gum to Koalas and feedback


The best indicator of gauging how well the gum is accepted by the koalas is through observing the koalas and communicating closely with the Senior Koala Keeper for keeper feedback.





When the koalas are given their fresh gum, a great indicator is if they appear animated, interested, and start eating straight away. However, koalas being koalas do sleep a lot so checking again in the morning after they have browsed through the evening and assess how popular the gum has been best.









Guide to gum feeding amounts


Cleland works on feeding two pieces of gum daily to each koala, preferably of varied species.
Presently we keep 85 koalas
85 x 2 pieces = 164 pieces a day
Rounded up to 170 a day
170 x 7 days = 1190 per week
Rounded up to 1200 pieces per week.
These totals will fluctuate as the numbers of koalas kept at the park vary.
You will always be guided by the Senior Koala keeper to the koala population numbers.
Gum tip numbers are in addition.
Tip is approx. 20 pieces a day of varied species depending on the season.
Weekly total approx. 140 tip pieces.

Collecting gum for unwell Koalas


When koalas become unwell, they become even more fussy. The keepers may ask for gum from a particular area or tree as they know it will help the individual koala that is in care. As tedious as this sometimes is, it is worth it to get the unwell koalas back to health.
Please work closely with the keepers when these situations occur.
